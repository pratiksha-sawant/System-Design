Tradeoffs are the interview.

Data engineering system design notes, built session by session. Nobody fails for not knowing what Kafka is — they fail when asked "why that over the alternative?" and the answer goes mushy. These notes build the one muscle that fixes it.

The core idea

Every design decision is a point on a spectrum

The left pole is almost always cheaper and simpler. The right pole buys speed or flexibility, paid for with complexity or risk. Your job in the room: pick a point, name what you're buying and paying, and argue the trade fits the requirement.

The reframe that wins rounds

A latency requirement is not a property of the data — it's a property of the decision the data serves. Same numbers, same dashboard: a retrospective decision tolerates a day of staleness; an in-flight decision tolerates minutes. When a stakeholder says "real-time," translate it into what decision does this drive, and how fast must it arrive to support that decision?

The repeatable method · tap each step

The five-step reasoning chain

1
Read the access pattern
Who consumes this, how often, and what does it reflect? Let consumption drive architecture — not the buzzword in the request.
›
2
Interrogate the requirement
Translate vague asks ("real-time") into the decision they serve, then into a concrete budget. Ask: "How late can this be and still let you act in time?"
›
3
Place on the spectrum
Pick the cheapest point that satisfies the decision. Watch both traps: don't over-reach to the right pole, don't under-serve a real need.
›
4
State the trade
Name what you're buying and what you're paying — one sentence each. You can't buy both poles on the same move.
›
5
Justify the trade
Argue the payment is cheap given the requirement. This clause turns a tradeoff into a defensible tradeoff.
›

The six recurring spectrums

Almost every decision you'll defend lives on one of these. Buying and paying are different on each — don't borrow vocabulary across them. Completed spectrums link to their own page.

Latency

Drilled
BatchStreaming
Batch buysSimplicity & low cost: one scheduled job, cheap compute, easy to debug and replay.
Streaming buysFreshness: seconds of latency — paid for with operational complexity and cost.

Schema

Schema-on-writeSchema-on-read
On-write buysValidation & reliability: bad data rejected at ingest. Cost: rigidity, slower iteration.
On-read buysFlexibility: dump raw, interpret later. Cost: risk — quality issues surface downstream.
Deep-dive coming soon → schema.html

Modeling

NormalizedDenormalized
Normalized buysNo duplication, one source of truth, cheap writes. Cost: more joins, slower reads.
Denormalized buysFast reads, fewer joins. Cost: duplicated data, harder/expensive updates.
Deep-dive coming soon → modeling.html

Pipeline

ETLELT
ETL buysClean, conformed data lands ready to use. Cost: rigid, transform upfront, reprocessing costly.
ELT buysLoad raw fast, transform in-warehouse on demand. Cost: raw storage + compute, governance harder.
Deep-dive coming soon → pipeline.html

Consistency

StrongEventual
Strong buysEvery read sees the latest write — correctness. Cost: higher latency, lower availability under partition.
Eventual buysAvailability & low latency at scale. Cost: reads can be stale; app must tolerate it.
