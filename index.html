Session 01 · Latency spectrum · 30 Jun 2026

Tradeoffs are the interview.

Data engineering system design notes. Nobody fails for not knowing what Kafka is — they fail when asked "why that over the alternative?" and the answer goes mushy. These notes build the one muscle that fixes it.

The core idea

Every design decision is a point on a spectrum

The left pole is almost always cheaper and simpler. The right pole buys speed or flexibility, paid for with complexity or risk. Your job in the room: pick a point, name what you're buying and paying, and argue the trade fits the requirement.

The reframe that wins rounds

The latency requirement is not a property of the data — it's a property of the decision the data serves. Same numbers, same dashboard: a retrospective decision tolerates a day of staleness; an in-flight decision tolerates minutes. When a stakeholder says "real-time," translate it into what decision does this drive, and how fast must it arrive to support that decision?

The repeatable method · tap each step

The five-step reasoning chain

1
Read the access pattern
Who consumes this, how often, and what does it reflect? Let consumption drive architecture — not the buzzword in the request.
›
2
Interrogate the requirement
Translate vague asks ("real-time") into the decision they serve, then into a concrete latency budget. Ask: "How late can the data be and still let you act in time?"
›
3
Place on the spectrum
Pick the cheapest point that satisfies the decision. Watch both traps: don't over-reach to streaming, don't under-serve an in-flight need.
›
4
State the trade
Name what you're buying and what you're paying — in one sentence each. You can't buy both poles on the same move.
›
5
Justify the trade
Argue the payment is cheap given the requirement. This clause turns a tradeoff into a defensible tradeoff.
›

The six recurring spectrums

Almost every decision you'll defend lives on one of these. Buying and paying are different on each — don't borrow vocabulary across them.

Latency

Drilled
BatchStreaming
Batch buysSimplicity & low cost: one scheduled job, cheap compute, easy to debug and replay.
Streaming buysFreshness: seconds of latency — paid for with operational complexity and cost.

Storage layout

Next
Row storeColumnar
Row buysFast single-record reads/writes — great for OLTP & point lookups.
Columnar buysFast scans & aggregation over few columns + compression — great for OLAP.

Schema

Schema-on-writeSchema-on-read
On-write buysValidation & reliability: bad data is rejected at ingest. Cost: rigidity, slower iteration.
On-read buysFlexibility: dump raw, interpret later. Cost: risk — quality issues surface downstream.

Modeling

NormalizedDenormalized
Normalized buysNo duplication, one source of truth, cheap writes. Cost: more joins, slower reads.
Denormalized buysFast reads, fewer joins. Cost: duplicated data, harder/expensive updates.

Pipeline

ETLELT
ETL buysClean, conformed data lands ready to use. Cost: rigid, transform logic upfront, reprocessing is costly.
ELT buysLoad raw fast, transform in-warehouse on demand. Cost: raw storage + warehouse compute, governance harder.

Consistency

StrongEventual
Strong buysEvery read sees the latest write — correctness. Cost: higher latency, lower availability under partition.
Eventual buysAvailability & low latency at scale. Cost: reads can be stale; app must tolerate it.
Deep dive · Latency · self-quiz

Scenarios — try before you reveal

Cover the answer, run the five steps out loud, then check yourself.

Scenario A · the original ask
A mid-size e-commerce company wants a dashboard of total revenue by product category that the finance team checks each morning to see how yesterday went. They ask you to "make it real-time."
Run all five steps. Where does it land, and how do you handle "real-time"?
  1. Access pattern: read once each morning, reflects yesterday — retrospective.
  2. Interrogate: ask "are you ever acting on this before the next day, or is the morning view of yesterday what you need?" — "real-time" collapses to "reliably updated by 8am."
  3. Place: Batch (far left). Daily scheduled job.
  4. Trade: buying simplicity & low cost; paying with freshness (always up to a day stale).
  5. Justify: staleness is free here — they only ever read yesterday's closed numbers.
"I'd go batch — a daily scheduled job. I'm buying simplicity and low cost; I'm paying with up to a day of staleness. That's the right trade, because finance reads yesterday's closed numbers each morning, so staleness costs them nothing."
Scenario B · the twist
Finance now runs intraday flash-sale promos. They want category revenue updating through the day so they can pull a promo that's losing money by mid-afternoon.
What changed in the requirement — and where does the answer move? (Trap: don't sprint to full streaming.)
  1. Access pattern: read in-flight, during the day — the decision is now "kill this promo now."
  2. Interrogate: "If a promo starts tanking, how fast must you see it — within the hour? 15 min?" That answer is your latency budget.
  3. Place: Micro-batch — a job every 15–30 min. Cheapest point that supports "react within the hour"; keeps batch's replayable, debuggable model. Not full streaming.
  4. Trade: buying freshness (a day → ~30 min); paying with more compute, steady source-system load, and tighter operational requirements.
  5. Justify: the data now has a clock — info about a bleeding promo is worth a lot at 2pm, worthless at 6pm. But "within the hour" doesn't justify streaming's cost.
"I'd move to micro-batch, every 15–30 min. I'm buying freshness — a day down to half an hour — because the decision is in-flight: stale data means lost money. I'm paying with more compute, steady source load, and tighter ops. I'd still avoid full streaming: 'react within the hour' doesn't justify the infrastructure."
Say it like this in the room

The three-beat answer template

→
What I chose. "I'd go [point on spectrum]…"
→
What it buys / costs. "…buying [X], paying with [Y]…"
→
Why it fits. "…and that's the right trade, because [the payment is cheap given the requirement]."

The third beat is what separates "I know the poles" from "I can defend a design." Most people stop after beat two.
